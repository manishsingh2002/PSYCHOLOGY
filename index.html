PILLAR 01: TRADING PSYCHOLOGY

COGNITIVE BIASES
(THE FLAWED BRAIN)

Nobel Prize-winning research (Kahneman & Tversky) proved that humans are inherently irrational. We feel the pain of a loss twice as intensely as the pleasure of a gain. This evolutionary trait creates disastrous trading behaviors.

PROSPECT THEORY (THE ASYMMETRY OF PAIN)LOSS AVERSION
Loss Aversion

Holding Losers

Because the pain of a loss is so extreme, the brain refuses to "realize" it by selling. Traders hold losing positions hoping they bounce back to breakeven, allowing a small paper cut to turn into an account-destroying hemorrhage.

The Disposition Effect

Selling Winners Early

Conversely, because the pleasure curve flattens out, the brain wants to lock in a guaranteed small win immediately rather than let it compound. Retail cuts winners short and lets losers run. Professionals do the exact opposite.

The Sunk Cost Fallacy

Averaging Down

"I've already lost $5,000, I can't quit now. Let me buy more to lower my average." The market does not care what your average price is. Averaging down into a structurally broken trend is the fastest way to blow up an account.

REFLEXIVITY
(THE SOROS LOOP)

George Soros made billions understanding one concept: Markets are not just observers of reality; they actively participate in changing it. Prices alter the fundamentals they are supposed to reflect.

The Positive Feedback Loop

How Bubbles Form

Traditional economics says Fundamentals drive Price. Reflexivity says Price drives Fundamentals. If a company's stock price skyrockets artificially, they can use that inflated stock to acquire competitors, borrow cheaper debt, and attract top talent—thus actually improving their fundamentals to match the fake price.

The Negative Feedback Loop

The Death Spiral

If a bank's stock drops due to an unfounded rumor, depositors see the stock crashing and panic, pulling their money out. The bank goes bankrupt. The rumor was false, but the dropping stock price caused the bankruptcy. Perception becomes reality. This is why you must respect price action.

PILLAR 02: PORTFOLIO MATHEMATICS

THE KELLY
CRITERION

How much of your portfolio should you risk on a single trade? Guessing is gambling. The Kelly Criterion is the exact mathematical formula used by quants and professional blackjack players to maximize long-term compound growth.

The Formula

K = W - [(1 - W) / R]

K: Percentage of capital to risk.
W: Win Probability (e.g., 50% or 0.50).
R: Risk/Reward Ratio (e.g., 2.0 for risking $1 to make $2).

Example: K = 0.50 - [(1 - 0.50) / 2.0]
K = 0.50 - [0.50 / 2.0] = 0.50 - 0.25 = 0.25 (25%)
The "Half-Kelly" Standard The Kelly formula assumes your inputs (win rate and reward) are perfectly accurate. In trading, they are just estimates. If you over-bet the Kelly Criterion, math dictates you will eventually go bankrupt. Professional funds use "Half-Kelly"—taking the formula's result and cutting it in half to ensure massive margin of safety against variance.
PILLAR 02: PORTFOLIO MATHEMATICS

ASSET ALLOCATION &
THE SHARPE RATIO

Ray Dalio calls it the "Holy Grail of Investing." Over 90% of your portfolio's variance is driven by what asset classes you hold (Stocks, Bonds, Gold), NOT by which specific stocks you picked.

THE EFFICIENT FRONTIER (MODERN PORTFOLIO THEORY)MATH OF DIVERSIFICATION
Uncorrelated Assets

The Free Lunch

If you combine two volatile assets that are uncorrelated (e.g., Stocks and Long-Term Government Bonds), the overall risk of the portfolio drops massively, but the returns do not. Diversification is the only "free lunch" in finance.

The Sharpe Ratio

Return / Volatility

Amateurs brag about absolute returns. Professionals care about Risk-Adjusted Returns. Calculated as (Return - Risk Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. A Sharpe ratio > 1.0 is excellent. Above 2.0 is elite.

Black Swan Hedging

Tail Risk Protection

Institutional funds buy deep Out-Of-The-Money Puts every month. 99% of the time, they expire worthless (insurance cost). When a Black Swan hits (2008, 2020), these puts explode 5000% in value, saving the portfolio.
